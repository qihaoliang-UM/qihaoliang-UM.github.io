About

I am completing a Bachelor of Arts in Sociology with Highest Honors at the University of Michigan, Ann Arbor (May 2026), with a minor in Anthropology. My honors thesis, advised by Professor Jaeeun Kim, examines how contested sovereignty between Mainland China and Taiwan structures educational migration, the conversion of credentials, and the boundaries of membership.

Methodologically, I work primarily in qualitative sociology — in-depth interviewing, fieldwork, and archival research — while also using R, Stata, and administrative datasets when the question calls for them. My broader interests sit at the intersection of migration studies, the sociology of the state and law, and East Asian Studies.

Before transferring to Michigan, I completed coursework toward a Bachelor of Laws in Political Science at Zhejiang University and spent a year as a Visiting Student in Sociology at UC Berkeley. I have held research assistantships at UC Berkeley, the University of Michigan, and Hamad Bin Khalifa University.

A Tale of Two Shores Contested Membership and Educational Migration between Mainland China and Taiwan

How does contested sovereignty reshape the value of cross-border education?

Scholarship on the internationalization of higher education often assumes that cross-border study loosens the ties among territory, membership, and educational value. This study shows that under contested sovereignty, cross-border study can instead make educational value more dependent on political classification and institutional recognition.

Drawing on 52 semi-structured interviews, fieldwork in Taiwan, and archival research, the analysis argues that cross-Strait educational migration is organized through a contested membership infrastructure: legal classifications, admissions systems, required documentation, recognition rules, and intermediary organizations. These arrangements permit enrollment yet also determine whether students can convert education into rights, recognized credentials, and future migration opportunities.

Many mainland Chinese families turn to Taiwan as a credential-repair route when gaokao outcomes close off better options; admission, however, does not secure stable study or a normal study-to-work-to-settlement pathway. Mainland Chinese students move through a fragile “two-key system,” and the value of Taiwan-earned education depends on where it is later evaluated. A comparison with Taiwanese students in mainland China shows that contested sovereignty produces differential inclusion and renders educational value deeply contingent on political classification and institutional recognition.

County Racial Distribution and Racial Disparities in Welfare Sanctions Group Threat and Contact Theories in California’s TANF System

Does local racial composition shape who gets sanctioned in U.S. welfare administration?

Drawing on California’s Cal-OAR dataset (2021–2023), this study employs ANOVA and multivariable regression to examine how county-level racial composition affects welfare sanction rates under Temporary Assistance for Needy Families. Sanction rates are elevated for Asian recipients, in tension with prevailing “model minority” framings; more racially balanced counties are associated with reduced sanction disparities, with effect sizes that vary by racial group.

Recognized by the Mark Chesler Student Research Award (2024), University of Michigan Department of Sociology.
